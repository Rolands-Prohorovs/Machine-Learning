### 1. Comparative Analysis (Benchmarking)

  What it is (simple):
 	You look at similar apps/websites and analyse:
     - How they work
 	 - What they do well
 	 - What is confusing or bad

This helps you not reinvent the wheel and understand what users already expect.

Use it when:
    - Before project starts
    - When you are planning features and structure

### 2. Observational Research (Contextual Observation)

  What it is:
 	You watch real people use similar systems or do similar tasks:
     - Where they struggle
 	 - Where they hesitate
 	 - How they actually behave (not what they say)

Very useful to see real-life problems.

Use it when:
    - Before project starts
    - Also useful if project exists but has usability problems

### 3. Interviews

  What it is:
 	You talk to users and ask about:
 	 - Their experience
 	 - Their problems
 	 - What they find annoying or difficult

Gives you deep understanding of user needs.


Use it when:
    - Before project starts (to understand needs)
    - Also during development (to validate ideas)

### 4. Probes / Diary Studies

  What it is:
 	Users write notes or record experiences over days/weeks:
 	 - When they use the system
 	 - What annoys them over time
 	 - What becomes important later

Good for understanding long-term usage.

Use it when:
    - When project already exists
    - Or when you have at least a prototype or real system

### 5. Focus Groups

  What it is:
 	You gather 5–8 users and discuss:
 	 - Ideas
 	 - Concepts
 	 - Opinions
 	 - What they like or dislike

Good for collecting opinions and priorities, not for precise testing.

Use it when:
    - Early stage (to discuss ideas)
    - Or mid-project (to choose between ideas)
